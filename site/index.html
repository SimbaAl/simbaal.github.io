About

From vehicular channels to the human gait

I am a postdoctoral researcher in the MUST Deep Learning group at North-West University, affiliated with the Centre for Artificial Intelligence Research (CAIR) and NITheCS. My research develops gradient-based attribution methods and structured compression techniques for deep neural networks applied to time-series problems.

My PhD focused on channel estimation for IEEE 802.11p vehicular communication, producing five peer-reviewed publications and three IEEE journal submissions currently under review. The postdoctoral work carries the same methodology into deep learning for prosthetics, including gait phase prediction, metabolic cost estimation and inter-subject domain adaptation for wearable sensing.

Beyond research, I serve as Communications Chairperson for the Deep Learning Indaba, the largest machine learning organisation in Africa, and lecture Signal Theory to third-year engineering students.

Doctoral research

Three methods, one thread

Vehicular wireless channels change faster than any classical estimator can comfortably track, and the hardware they run on has no room to spare. My doctoral work makes deep channel estimators accurate, explainable and small, in that order.

DPA-RDCNN

vehicular channel estimation

A temporal convolutional architecture with data pilot-aided refinement for IEEE 802.11p channels. It tracks fast vehicular fading across six standard channel models and runs at 4.16 ms single-frame latency, fast enough for real deployment.

Read the paper →

REACH

gradient-based interpretability

An interpretability framework that identifies which features and filters a channel estimator actually relies on, then turns that evidence into a principled guide for architecture compression across channel conditions.

Read the preprint →

TurboQuant

attribution-guided compression

Joint interpretability-guided pruning and near-optimal Lloyd-Max weight quantisation for deep vehicular channel estimators, shrinking models for edge deployment while preserving bit error rate performance.

Read the paper →

Postdoctoral research

The same mathematics, walking

A vehicular channel and a walking human are both fast-changing time-series measured by imperfect sensors. My postdoctoral work carries interpretability and compression methods from wireless communication into deep learning for prosthetics and exoskeletons.

Gait phase prediction

Multi-source domain adaptation that lets gait models transfer across subjects, so a prosthetic controller trained on one group of people generalises to a new wearer.

Metabolic cost estimation

Predicting the energetic cost of walking from wearable sensors such as EMG and joint kinematics, working towards assistive devices that adapt to how hard the wearer is actually working.
